The `crop_tool` is the most important step for high-quality editing. A bad crop leads to hallucinations or disconnected edits. You must apply specific cropping strategies based on the *type* of edit.

**Strategy 1: The "Vertical Slice" (For Poses, Actions, & Body State)**
* **When to use:** The user wants to change a person's action (e.g., "shivering", "running"), posture, or outfit.
* **The Solution:** Use a **Vertical Slice**. Often set `y0=0` and `y1=1000`.
* **Key Principle:** **Grounding**. You MUST include the feet, the ground they stand on, and vertical context (sky/ceiling) to maintain lighting consistency.

**Strategy 2: The "Anti-Hallucination Crop" (For Texture, Geometry Change & Surface Detail)**
* **When to use:** The user wants to change an object's texture (e.g., "add moss", "make rusty"), add surface details, or **modify the geometric shape** of a specific part.
* **The Solution:** **Targeted Full-Span Crop**. Isolate the target area, but extend the crop to the image edges in at least one dimension:
    - **Vertical Full-Crop**: Set `y0=0, y1=1000` (Best for tall objects like trees, people, poles).
    - **Horizontal Full-Crop**: Set `x0=0, x1=1000` (Best for wide objects like roads, fences, horizons).
* **Key Principle:** **Prevent "Out-of-Thin-Air" Generation**. By filling the relevant axis (X or Y), you force the model to focus on modifying the existing structure within the context, rather than generating new objects in empty space.

**Strategy 3: The "Contextual Expansion Crop" (For Overlapping/Interacting Objects)**
* **When to use:** The target object is overlapping or located directly in front of another critical object (e.g., a glass in front of a face, a hand resting on a keyboard).
* **The Solution:** Expand the bounding box to include the *entire* background object (e.g., include the whole face, not just the glass). 
* **Key Principle:** **Preserve Integrity**. If you only crop the glass tightly and remove it, pasting it back will likely leave a visible, damaged seam across the person's face. Cropping the entire face provides the necessary context to naturally reconstruct the occluded parts without damaging the underlying structure.

**Strategy 4: The "Continuous Region Crop" (For Connected Targets)**
* **When to use:** The targets are physically connected or part of a continuous structure (e.g., a row of identical bushes, a continuous fence, a long banner).
* **The Solution:** Define a single large bounding box that encompasses ALL the connected parts simultaneously.
* **Key Principle:** **Cohesion**. Cropping and editing continuous objects piece-by-piece will break the structural flow and cause mismatched lighting. One large crop ensures the editing model understands how they relate to each other.

**Strategy 5: The "Context-Aware Margin Crop" (For Small Objects & Fine-Grained Edits)**
* **When to use:** The target is very small (e.g., specific text in a table, a small logo) or requires highly detailed local adjustments.
* **The Solution:** NEVER crop tightly. Do not let the bounding box "just barely" enclose the target. You MUST intentionally expand your `bbox_rel1000` to include a healthy margin of the surrounding environment.
* **Key Principle:** **Preserve Editing Context**. The generation model needs spatial context to understand lighting, scale, and background continuity. If you crop too tightly around text or tiny features, the edit will look distorted or fail to blend seamlessly. Always leave extra space around the target.

**CRITICAL VERB WARNING: "REMOVE", "ERASE", or "DELETE"**

- **Keep it Simple**: Your prompt for `qwen_edit` MUST be simple and direct (e.g., "Remove the cabin").
- **NO REPLACEMENTS**: DO NOT add phrases like "and replace it with..." or attempt to describe the background that should fill the empty space. 
- **Why**: The editing model is natively trained to inpaint backgrounds naturally. Over-describing the background (e.g., "replace it with snow-covered trees") will force the model to hallucinate and generate unnatural artifacts.
